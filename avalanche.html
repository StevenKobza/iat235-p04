What are Avalanches?

"Snow is deposited in successive layers as the winter progresses. These layers may have dissimilar physical properties and an avalanche occurs when one layer slides on another (Surface Avalanche), or the whole snow cover slides on the ground (Full-Depth). An avalanche may be Dry or Wet, according to whether free water is present in the snow. It may be of Loose Snow, when the avalanche starts at a single point, or a Slab Avalanche which occurs when an area of more cohesive snow separates from the surrounding snow and slides out. In practice, any snow slide big enough to carry a person down, is important. Avalanche configurations are illustrated in the SAIS "Avalanche Recording Form"." (Winterdata, pg. 3)

How To Increase Your Odds of Surviving an Avalanche

  • Plunge an ice axe into the undersurface. This may help you near the top of the slide.

  • Shout. Others may hear you.

  • Get rid of gear; sacks, skis, etc

If You Are Caught in an Avalanche

  • Keep one hand in front of your face and try to maintain air space.

  • Try to main space for chest expansion by taking and holding a deep breath

  • Try to avoid panic and conserve energy. Your companions are probably searching for you.

Hazardous Terrain to avoid

Gullies

Gullies are similar to large ditches or small valleys, they are land that has been erroded by passing water.

Convex Slopes

Convex slopes are slopes that start off with a shallow decline but as you pass over the edge, the slope rapidly falls off and it looks like a 90 degree surface

Lee Slopes

Lee slopes are slopes that are downwind on the ridge. The dangerous part of these slopes is that there can be Cornices or ledges of snow which tells the skiier that it's safe when in reality there is no support underneath them.
